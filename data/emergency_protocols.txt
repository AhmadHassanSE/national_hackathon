PROTOCOL: Cardiac Arrest

1. Confirm scene safety before touching the patient. Look for traffic, exposed wires, fire, violence, gas, water, or unstable debris. Do not become a second casualty. If the scene is unsafe, move back, warn others, and call emergency services while waiting for trained responders.

2. Check responsiveness by tapping the shoulders firmly and speaking loudly: "Are you okay?" or "Aap theek hain?" If there is no response, shout for help and assign one person to call an ambulance. If bystanders are present, point to a specific person and say: "You call emergency services now and come back." Ask another person to look for an AED if one may be nearby.

3. Place the patient flat on their back on a firm surface. Open the airway by tilting the head back and lifting the chin, unless major trauma is strongly suspected. Check breathing for no more than 10 seconds. Look for chest rise, listen for air movement, and feel for breathing. Gasping, snoring, or occasional abnormal breaths are not normal breathing.

4. If the patient is unresponsive and not breathing normally, start CPR immediately. Kneel beside the chest. Put the heel of one hand in the center of the chest on the lower half of the breastbone, place the other hand on top, lock elbows, and keep shoulders directly over hands.

5. Push hard and fast at a rate of 100 to 120 compressions per minute. Compress the adult chest about 5 to 6 cm and allow full recoil after every compression. Minimize interruptions. If trained and willing to give rescue breaths, use 30 compressions followed by 2 breaths. If not trained, continue hands-only CPR without stopping.

6. If an AED arrives, turn it on and follow voice prompts. Expose and dry the chest. Attach pads as shown on the diagrams. Make sure nobody touches the patient while the AED analyzes rhythm or delivers a shock. Resume CPR immediately after the shock or if no shock is advised.

7. Continue CPR until the patient shows clear signs of life, trained help takes over, an AED instructs you otherwise, the scene becomes unsafe, or you are physically unable to continue. If multiple helpers are present, switch compressors every 2 minutes to maintain depth and speed.

8. Contraindications and cautions: Do not delay CPR to search for a pulse if you are untrained or uncertain. Do not give water, food, tablets, or oral medication to an unconscious person. Do not slap, shake violently, or try to make the patient stand. Do not stop compressions because ribs crack; continue if the patient remains unresponsive and not breathing normally. Do not perform blind finger sweeps in the mouth. If vomiting occurs, roll the patient briefly to the side, clear visible material, return them to their back, and restart CPR.

PROTOCOL: Severe Bleeding

1. Confirm scene safety first. Look for traffic, sharp metal, broken glass, electrical hazards, fire, chemicals, violence, or unstable structures. Put on gloves if available. If gloves are not available, use plastic bags, clean cloth, or layered fabric as a barrier. Call emergency services early if bleeding is heavy, spurting, soaking clothing, caused by major trauma, or the patient is pale, confused, weak, or faint.

2. Expose the wound enough to identify where blood is coming from. Do not waste time fully undressing the patient. If there is an obvious object embedded in the wound, do not pull it out. Apply pressure around the object and stabilize it with bulky cloth or dressings.

3. Apply direct pressure immediately. Place clean gauze, a clean towel, folded cloth, or clothing directly over the bleeding site. Press firmly with the palm of your hand. Maintain constant pressure for at least 10 minutes without repeatedly lifting the dressing to check. If blood soaks through, add more layers on top and continue pressure. Do not remove the original soaked layer because it may disturb clotting.

4. Position the patient lying down if possible. Keep them still and warm. If there is no suspected fracture, severe pain, or spinal injury, raise the bleeding limb above heart level while maintaining direct pressure. Reassure the patient and monitor breathing, skin color, alertness, and pulse if you know how.

5. If bleeding from an arm or leg remains life-threatening despite firm direct pressure, apply a tourniquet if trained and available. Place it 5 to 7 cm above the wound, never directly over a joint. Tighten until bleeding stops, secure it, and record the application time. Once applied for severe bleeding, do not loosen it while waiting for medical care.

6. For wounds where a tourniquet cannot be applied, such as groin, armpit, shoulder, or neck-adjacent wounds, pack the wound if trained. Push sterile gauze or clean cloth firmly into the wound cavity and maintain direct pressure. Use hemostatic gauze if available. For neck bleeding, use pressure carefully and avoid blocking the airway or both carotid arteries.

7. Treat for shock. Keep the patient flat, warm, and calm. Do not give food, drink, cigarettes, or oral medicines because surgery or anesthesia may be needed. If the patient becomes unconscious but is breathing normally, place them in the recovery position unless spinal injury is suspected. If breathing stops, begin CPR.

8. Contraindications and cautions: Do not use powder, soil, coffee, turmeric, ash, toothpaste, or household chemicals on the wound. Do not wash severe active bleeding before pressure is controlled. Do not remove embedded objects. Do not delay ambulance activation while searching for perfect supplies. Do not apply a tourniquet for minor bleeding that can be controlled with pressure. Do not place a tourniquet over the neck, torso, or a joint. Do not allow the patient to walk around after major blood loss.
